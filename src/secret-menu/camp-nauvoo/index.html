A Long-Term Vision · A Personal Project

Eighty-Six Acres of Sierra Forest, Reimagined as a Place to Heal

A long-term plan to acquire Camp Nauvoo, an existing 86-acre camp in the El Dorado County foothills, and run it as a nonprofit. Three missions under one canopy of trees: a respite for burned-out healthcare workers, a quiet place for people near the end of life to spend their last good days outdoors, and communal camping open to everyone that would keep the whole thing running.

Why Buy a Camp

Three missions, one piece of land

The land does the work that buildings cannot. Each program below uses the same forest, the same creek, and the same lodge, just for different people at different moments in their lives.

01
Respite for healthcare workers
For the people who hold everyone else together
Healthcare workers carry the weight of everyone else's worst days. Burnout and moral injury are not personal weaknesses, they are occupational injuries, and they almost never get treated like one. This program would offer multi-day stays in the forest for clinicians who are running on empty. No pager, no charting, no expectations. Just rest, quiet, and the room to remember why they started.
02
A final stay in the open air
Last good days spent outdoors, not under fluorescent lights
For people near the end of life, the final weeks are too often spent in a hospital room. This program would invite terminal patients and their families to spend a few days here instead. In the trees, beside the creek, in the open air. A calm and dignified setting to gather, to rest, and to say what needs saying, away from monitors and corridors. Coordinated alongside hospice and palliative care, never in place of it.
03
Communal camping, open to everyone
The public campground that keeps it all running
The camp would keep operating as a working public campground. Families, groups, and travelers would book the lodging and tent sites just as they do today. That revenue is what would make the two charitable programs possible without depending entirely on donations, and it would keep the land alive with people through the season rather than sitting empty.
Why This, Why Now

The need is documented, and expensive

This is not a feel-good extra. Health-worker burnout is a measured, costly workforce problem, which is exactly why employers and health systems have a real reason to help fund the respite side of this, not just goodwill.

A system that keeps even a handful more clinicians a year more than covers the price of sending them here, and the people who hold those retention budgets are exactly the partners this needs.

Room to Grow

Where the revenue compounds

The statement above is what the land earns today, on a fraction of its potential and almost entirely from weekend leisure travel. Eighty-six acres leaves real room to add capacity and, more importantly, a second revenue stream that does not depend on the weather or the weekend.

01 // NEW STREAM
Regional care-center day trips
Partner with adult day programs, assisted living and memory care, rehab centers, and veterans' groups across the Sacramento and foothill region to bring residents out for guided days in the forest. It is contracted, repeatable, and lands midweek and off-season, exactly when leisure camping is quiet. A steady B2B base underneath the seasonal leisure revenue.
02 // FAST PAYBACK
More RV sites
RV is already the highest-yielding site type on the property at about $176 a night, well above the tent pads. The acreage and existing access support adding hookup sites, the quickest-payback expansion available and the one that needs the least new construction.
03 // HIGHER CEILING
Cabins and glamping
A few cabins or safari tents raise the nightly ceiling far above a tent pad. The tailwind is real: the U.S. glamping market hit roughly $738M in 2024 and is growing about 12% a year, with Marriott, Hilton, and Hyatt all moving in. Demand for exactly this kind of stay is climbing.
04 // THE LAND ITSELF
Trails and gathering spaces
Investing in the miles of existing trails, signage, and quiet spots beside the creek raises what every guest gets out of a stay, leisure or clinical, and directly supports the day-trip programs. The land is the product, and a little improvement goes a long way across 86 acres.

The Model

How a campground funds a charity

The structure is what would make this durable. Earned revenue from public camping would carry the fixed costs of the land, which lets philanthropic funds go almost entirely toward the people the programs are meant to serve.

01 // EARN
Public camping generates revenue
Open-season bookings from families and groups would cover property taxes, insurance, utilities, and grounds upkeep. The land would pay to keep its own lights on.
02 // SUBSIDIZE
Free and low-cost stays for the mission
Off-peak windows and dedicated cabins would be reserved for healthcare-worker respite stays and end-of-life retreats, offered free or at cost to the people who need them.
03 // SUSTAIN
Donations and grants extend the reach
With operating costs covered by camping income, philanthropic dollars and grants would stretch much further and go directly toward serving more guests.
The Roadmap

How it comes together, year by year

Nothing here rests on a single leap. The camp already operates, so the plan is to acquire first, then layer on the mission and the new revenue as the numbers allow.

Year 0 · Acquire
Buy the land, keep it running
Close on the 86 acres, stand up the nonprofit, and keep the existing camping operation running without a gap. Day one, it is already a working business.
Year 1 · Stabilize
Tighten ops, pilot the mission
Sharpen the camping numbers, run the first healthcare-worker respite stays in the off-season, and start co-designing the end-of-life retreat with a hospice partner.
Year 2 · Add revenue
Open the second income stream
Launch the regional care-center day-trip partnerships, add RV sites, and stage the first access-road improvements out of earned revenue.
Year 3 · Scale
Grow what works
Add cabin or glamping capacity, formalize employer respite contracts, and expand the charitable programs as earned revenue and donations grow.
The Long Arc

Where this goes, in scale and in revenue

The roadmap gets the camp on its feet. This is the horizon past it. Growth here does not mean borrowing big and hoping. It means using more of the land we already own, serving more people across more of the year, and stacking revenue so no single stream has to carry the whole thing.

Growing the scale
01 // THE LAND WE HAVE
More on the 86 acres
Today the camp uses a sliver of what it sits on. Twenty sites on eighty-six acres. The land has room for more cabins, more RV hookups, glamping pads, and gathering spaces, added a few at a time out of what the camp already earns. We grow into the acreage. We do not borrow against it.
02 // SAME LAND, MORE MISSION
Deeper programs, same forest
Scale is not only square footage. The same trees can hold longer respite cohorts, structured retreats, training days, and family end-of-life stays running side by side. More people served, more weeks a year, on land we already own.
03 // BEYOND THE SEASON
A year-round operation
Leisure camping is a summer story. The mission is not. Winterized cabins and the indoor lodge let respite stays, care-center day trips, and retreats run in the off-season, exactly when leisure camping goes quiet. The goal is a place that works in November, not just July.
04 // FILL WHAT EXISTS
Reach, utilization, partnerships
The fastest growth costs the least: filling the nights we already have. Real marketing, a booking presence that actually converts, and strategic partnerships with health systems, hospices, and regional care programs turn empty midweek dates into booked ones. Higher utilization before a single new structure goes up.

Straight Talk

The honest weak spot, and why it is manageable

Any serious partner will ask, so here it is without spin. The property's main limitation is the road in.

Access: the driveway in
The entrance road is narrow and steep in spots, the kind of first impression that gives a visitor pause, and it is the single biggest knock on the site. Here is what takes the edge off it: the access is already licensed and grandfathered for the camp's existing uses. The operation runs on it today as a licensed campground and wedding venue, so the business is not waiting on new approvals to function. Improvements can be staged over time as revenue allows, rather than standing as a gate that has to be cleared before anything else can happen. A known, bounded, already-permitted limitation is a very different thing from an unknown one.
