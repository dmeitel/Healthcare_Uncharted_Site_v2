Article 03 The Provider

It's not the interface. It's the filing system.

Every clinician has a moment, usually around click forty of documenting one visit, of asking who designed this thing and whether they ever met a patient. Fair question. The answer is that the screen you fight with every shift is the visible tip of a data architecture that is older than most of the people using it, and that almost none of it is arbitrary.

Principle The EHR is not a documentation tool that happens to bill. It is a billing and legal record system that happens to document care. Every "why does it work this way" answer starts there.
By David Eitel ~12 min read ONC · HL7 · Arndt
EHR Systems Clinical Informatics FHIR & Interop
The shape

Patient, encounter, event, data point. The chain isn't a design choice. It's forced by medicine, billing law, and thirty years of regulation stacking up.

The split

The database clinicians write to is not the database analysts read from. That gap, and the overnight bridge across it, is why your dashboard describes yesterday.

The cost

Primary care physicians spend over half the workday inside the EHR. The workflow is the architecture, surfaced one click at a time.

The chain

Everything hangs off something

Start with the structure, because every screen you have ever used is a window onto it. When registration turns a person into a record, the process I walked through in Article 02, that record becomes the root of a tree. Everything else in the chart hangs off it in a strict order.

Level 1
Patient

One identity, one root. The thing the whole tree exists to keep straight.

Level 2
Encounter

The visit container. Nothing clinical exists without one, because nothing BILLABLE exists without one.

Level 3
Order / Event

The med, the lab, the imaging study. Each one hangs off exactly one encounter.

Level 4
Data point

The result, the vital, the note. Each one hangs off the event that produced it.

Why an encounter, though? Why can't a lab result just belong to the patient? Because the payment system needs to know which VISIT to attach the claim to, and the legal record needs to know under whose care and on whose authority everything happened. The encounter is where medicine, billing, and liability all agree to meet. Once you see that, half the EHR's rigidity explains itself: the system will fight you to the death before it lets clinical data float free of its container.

And here's Conway's law doing pushups in the background: the software shipped the org chart. Registration owns level one, clinical departments own level three, and the seams between them are exactly where your workflow gets weird.

The filing cabinet

Older than relational

Now the part almost nobody tells clinicians. The major EHRs are not built like modern web apps, and they are not built like the relational databases every analyst learns first. Their roots go back to MUMPS, a hierarchical database and language built at Massachusetts General Hospital in the 1960s, DESIGNED for medical records before "database" was even a settled idea. The dominant systems in American hospitals still carry that lineage in their bones.

A hierarchical store is built for one question: give me EVERYTHING about THIS patient, fast. Top of the tree, straight down, whole chart, milliseconds. That is exactly the question a clinician at the bedside asks, and it is why the chart opens instantly at 3 AM even when the hospital's network is having a bad night. I work in this architecture every day on the informatics side, and for its intended question it remains ruthlessly good.

But flip the question sideways, "every patient whose creatinine doubled in 48 hours," and the filing cabinet becomes the wrong tool. That question wants to cut ACROSS every tree at once, and a chart-shaped store answers it badly. So the industry evolved a standard pattern: keep the fast hierarchical chart for care, and copy everything out, on a schedule, into relational databases built for exactly those sideways questions.

The pointThe EHR isn't slow-minded. It's optimized for a different question than the one analysts ask. One patient deep, not all patients wide. Every reporting frustration you've ever had lives in that difference.
The split

Why your dashboard describes yesterday

That copy-out is the split brain of every hospital's data life, and it is the single most useful thing a clinician can know about reporting. The chart you documented in at 2 PM is the operational system. The report your manager pulls is running against the relational copy, and that copy was refreshed overnight. Between the two sits an extract pipeline with a schedule, a lag, and occasionally a bad morning.

So when the unit dashboard contradicts what you KNOW happened on your shift, nobody is lying to you. You are looking at two different databases, one of which is describing yesterday. Ask which one your report runs against. Watch how few people can answer.

The chart is real time. The report is a photograph of the chart, developed overnight.

This split is also why the site's SQL Mystery game exists, it teaches the reporting side's shape, and why Method 02 spends so much time on where numbers actually come from. If you work WITH healthcare data rather than IN the chart, the relational copy is your whole world, encounter-grained, and it inherits every quirk of the chain above.

The workflow

Regulation, rendered in buttons

So the architecture explains the shape. What explains the CLICKS? Mostly law, arriving in layers. In 2009 the HITECH Act put $25.9 billion behind EHR adoption, and it worked; nearly every American hospital went digital in half a decade. But the money came with Meaningful Use criteria, and the criteria came with ONC certification requirements, and every one of those requirements had to land somewhere in the interface. A measurable share of the screens you click through exist because a regulation said the system must capture that field, and a certification test verified the button was there.

Layer on billing. For decades, evaluation-and-management coding rules tied reimbursement to what was DOCUMENTED, so documentation grew to serve the claim. The 4Ps lens from Article 01 earns its keep right here: the Provider holds the keyboard, but the screen serves the Payer and the Policymaker too. Three masters, one interface, and the clinician pays the integration cost.

That cost is measured. Arndt and colleagues instrumented primary care physicians' EHR use and found they spend more than half the workday in the system, a finding that has anchored the documentation-burden literature since. It is also why ambient scribes stuck when so much health tech didn't, which is a story I told in Article 07.

The pointThe system is not gaslighting you. It is faithfully serving customers who aren't in the room: the claim, the auditor, the certification test. Name the customer behind a screen and the screen starts making sense.
The door

FHIR, and what's opening

One more piece, because it's the hopeful one. For most of EHR history, getting data OUT meant proprietary interfaces and expensive middleware. The FHIR standard changed the posture: both major vendors now publish public API documentation, at fhir.epic.com and fhir.cerner.com, describing standardized resources any authorized app can request. The 21st Century Cures Act's information-blocking rules put federal weight behind that openness. The atlas has a tile for it, because the fight over that door is one of the quiet big stories in health IT.

It does not rewrite the filing cabinet. The chain is still the chain; FHIR resources mirror it almost exactly, which by this point in the article should not surprise you. What it changes is who gets to ask the cabinet questions. That is new, and it is why this era of health IT is more interesting than the decade before it.

If you work in a hospital: the system you fight is a forty-year-old answer to a question that hasn't changed, wrapped in twenty years of regulation, finally growing standard doors. Fight it less. Learn its grain. The grain is learnable, and once you have it, you stop being the person the system happens TO.
