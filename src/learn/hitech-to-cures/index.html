Article 05 The policy makers

Paid to adopt. Forced to share.

In 2008, nine percent of American hospitals had a basic EHR. Washington wrote checks for over $35 billion and got near-total adoption inside a decade. It also got two hours of desk work per hour of patient care, and data that STILL would not move between buildings. So Congress passed another law to free the data it had paid to create.

Principle The screen you fight with every shift is not an accident of software. It is the output of an incentive structure, built in two acts: HITECH paid for adoption, Cures penalized hoarding. Understand the incentives and the EHR's shape stops being mysterious.
By David Eitel ~12 min read ONC · CMS · GAO · Federal Register
Federal Health Reform Medical Record Tools Interoperability
The carrot

HITECH worked, as measured. $35B+ in incentive payments, 400,000+ clinicians paid, hospital adoption from 9% to virtually 100%. The causal studies confirm the money did it.

The bill

Nobody paid for usable. Two hours of EHR and desk work per hour of patient care. Usability scores in the bottom 9% of all rated technology. Notes four times longer than other countries'.

The stick

Cures made hoarding illegal. Information blocking now carries penalties up to $1M per violation for vendors, real disincentives for providers, and a mandatory FHIR API in every certified EHR.

Before the money

Paper was an equilibrium

Rewind to 2008. A landmark NEJM survey found 1.5% of U.S. hospitals with a comprehensive electronic record and 7.6% with even a basic one. Computerized order entry lived in 17% of hospitals. On the clinic side, 4% of physicians had a fully functional EHR. This was not because clinicians loved paper. Both major surveys found the same blocker: money. Capital costs, maintenance costs, and no financial return, because the efficiency gains of digitization mostly landed on payers and patients, not on the people buying the software.

That is a market equilibrium, and equilibria do not move on their own. Moving one takes a shock. The shock arrived in February 2009, riding inside the stimulus bill.

The carrot

HITECH: adoption, purchased

The HITECH Act, Title XIII of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, did something health policy rarely does: it wrote checks to individual clinicians and hospitals for buying and USING certified software. Up to $44,000 per physician through Medicare, up to $63,750 through Medicaid, a $2 million base payment per hospital plus per-discharge add-ons. To collect, you had to demonstrate "meaningful use": Stage 1 in 2011 asked you to capture and share data, Stage 2 in 2014 raised the thresholds and demanded exchange, Stage 3 aimed at outcomes. From 2015, the checks turned into penalties: Medicare fee cuts for clinicians, reduced market-basket updates for hospitals that stayed analog.

Did the money cause the adoption, or just ride the wave? The cleanest natural experiment says caused: hospitals ELIGIBLE for the incentives accelerated adoption by 14.2 percentage points a year after the program began, against 3.3 points for ineligible hospitals. An eleven-point policy effect, visible from orbit. By 2015, 84% of hospitals had at least a basic EHR and 96% possessed certified technology. Office-based physicians went from 17% to 78% by 2021, then kept climbing to 91% in 2024. As an adoption program, HITECH is one of the most effective interventions in the history of American health policy.

Hold on to the phrase "as an adoption program." The program's own metric is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

The bill

What adoption didn't buy

The incentives measured adoption and attested checkbox use. So that is what the market optimized. Certified criteria became feature lists, feature lists became screens, and the screens landed on clinicians. The measurements of what happened next are some of the most cited numbers in medicine. Sinsky's time-motion study: for every hour of direct clinical face time, nearly TWO additional hours of EHR and desk work, plus the nightly pajama-time session from home. Shanafelt's survey of 6,375 physicians tied order entry use to 29% higher odds of burnout. When physicians graded EHR usability on the standard scale used across industries, the score landed in the bottom 9% of all rated technologies. An F, from the people required by federal policy to use it.

The documentation itself mutated. American ambulatory notes run about four times longer than notes in other countries. Epic's own cross-country metadata shows U.S. clinicians spending 90 minutes a day of active EHR time against 59 outside the U.S., with far more of the note auto-generated. Billing requirements and quality attestation wrote themselves into the clinical record, because the record is where the incentives pointed. If you want the general law behind this, it has a name: Goodhart's. When a measure becomes a target, it stops measuring.

The incentives measured adoption, so adoption is what we got. Nobody was paid for usable.

And the data still didn't move. ONC's 2015 report to Congress put a name on the quiet part: information blocking. Vendors charging cost-prohibitive fees for interfaces and data export, contracts discouraging exchange, most complaints pointing at the developers of certified health IT. The taxpayer had funded the digitization of American medicine and then discovered the records were digital the way a safe-deposit box is portable.

The stick

Cures: hoarding made illegal

The 21st Century Cures Act, December 2016, is remembered for drug approval reform. Its durable health IT legacy is section 4004: the information blocking prohibition. The 2020 final rule turned it into regulation with three classes of regulated actors, providers, certified health IT developers, and exchange networks, a short list of exceptions, and a redesigned certification program whose centerpiece is a standardized FHIR API in every certified EHR. Patient-facing apps stopped being a vendor courtesy and became a certification condition.

Apr 2021
Information blocking rules take effect. Interfering with access or exchange of electronic health information is now a regulatory violation, not a business model.
Dec 2022
The FHIR API deadline. Certified EHRs must ship the standardized patient-and-population API. The EHI definition expands past the core data set.
Sep 2023
Enforcement begins for vendors and networks. OIG civil money penalties up to $1 million PER VIOLATION.
Jul 2024
Provider disincentives finalized. Offending hospitals lose three quarters of their annual payment update. MIPS clinicians zero out their interoperability score. ACOs get barred from Shared Savings.
2023-2025
TEFCA goes live. The Cures-mandated national exchange framework designates its first networks; eleven QHINs now connect the major EHR vendors, networks, and Surescripts under one agreement.

Different tool, different target. HITECH paid individual organizations to digitize themselves. Cures regulates the CONNECTIONS between them, with penalties instead of checks. Two acts, one lesson in policy design: you can buy behavior inside an institution, but moving data across institutional boundaries required making the alternative illegal.

The scoreboard

Where it stands

The honest scoreboard, from the government's own data. Adoption: effectively done, 99.4% of hospitals. Exchange: genuinely moving, 70% of hospitals now engage in all four interoperability domains at least sometimes, up from 46% in 2018. But only 43% do it ROUTINELY, which means the average hospital still treats interoperability as an occasional activity seventeen years after the first checks went out.

And the map has holes exactly where the money never went. Behavioral health clinicians were largely ineligible for HITECH, and it shows: 67% of psychiatric hospitals hold certified EHRs against 86% of general hospitals, and the settings range from 52% to 96% depending on who funds them. Skilled nursing facilities: two thirds adopted, but in the last full federal survey only 18% could integrate an outside record. The catch-up funding, a $20 million behavioral health IT initiative, is roughly one twentieth of one percent of what the eligible side received. The two-tier system is not a metaphor. It is a line item.

Meanwhile the policy machinery itself keeps moving. ONC became ASTP in 2024, then became ONC again in 2026. The follow-on HTI-2 rule was proposed, partially finalized, and then largely withdrawn in favor of a deregulatory proposal. Whatever your politics, the operational lesson for anyone running health IT is stable: the certification criteria, data standards, and enforcement posture underneath your EHR are POLICY VARIABLES, and they move. Plan like it.
