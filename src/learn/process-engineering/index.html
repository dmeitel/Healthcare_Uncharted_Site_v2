Method Process engineering

Nobody designed this.

Every workflow in healthcare is a decision somebody made. Most of them, nobody made on purpose. They accreted, one workaround at a time, until the workaround was the process. Process engineering is the discipline of building the work on purpose, and this whole site is one long attempt to do it.

Principle You cannot fix a process you have not mapped. You cannot map one you refuse to look at. So you look. That is most of the job, and almost nobody does it.
By David Eitel ~12 min read Ohno · Deming · Goldratt

The premise

It all accreted.

Walk a hospital floor at 6 a.m. and watch the morning med pass. Watch a discharge that should take an hour take six. Watch a prior authorization bounce between a nurse, a fax machine that is somehow still a fax machine, and an insurance portal that times out. Then ask the obvious question. Who designed this?

Nobody did. Not really. A scheduling rule got added after a bad week in 2014. A double-check got bolted on after a near miss. A field became required because one auditor asked for it once. None of it was drawn on a single sheet of paper by a person who could see the whole thing. It accreted. And accreted process is the default state of almost every organization, healthcare more than most.

"A workaround that survives long enough stops being a workaround. It becomes the process. Then it gets trained. Audited. Defended."

Process engineering is the opposite of accretion. It is the discipline of taking a system that produces some outcome and redesigning the steps so it produces a better one. On purpose. With evidence. The idea did not come from medicine. It came from factories, from Taiichi Ohno on the Toyota shop floor working out why a car took longer to build than the sum of its parts. It came from W. Edwards Deming teaching variation and feedback to postwar Japan. It came from a physicist named Goldratt asking why a plant with no idle machines still could not ship on time.

Healthcare imported all of it. Late in some places, badly in a few, and in a handful of cases brilliantly. The point of this piece is to show you the toolkit, then show you that the toolkit is exactly what the rest of this site is built out of. The maps are the method.

The first move

Map before you fix.

Here is the mistake almost everyone makes. They feel the pain, they name a cause, and they jump to a fix. The discharge is slow, so we will add a discharge coordinator. The fix lands on top of an unmapped process, and six months later the discharge is still slow and now there is a coordinator too.

The engineer does something slower and far more useful first. They draw the actual flow. Not the flow in the policy binder. The real one. Who touches the work, in what order, and what has to be handed from one person to the next. The notation for that is old and boring and it works: the swim lane. One lane per role. The work moves left to right. Every time a box crosses a lane line, that is a handoff.

A visit, drawn honestly
Swim-lane map
Every lane boundary is a handoff, and every handoff is where work waits, drops, or dies. The amber path is the one that stalls a real visit for days. The clinical work was fast. The crossing into the payer lane and back is where the time went. You only see that once you draw it.

That picture is doing a lot of work. It tells you the clinical steps were never the bottleneck. It tells you the time lives in a crossing between two organizations that do not share a system. And it gives you something to fix that is real, instead of adding another box to a process you could not see.

Mature versions of this get names and standards. SIPOC forces you to write down the Suppliers, Inputs, Process, Outputs, and Customers before you map a single step, so you do not start in the middle. BPMN, business process model and notation, is the formal grammar of these diagrams, the one analysts use so two people draw the same symbol the same way. You do not need the jargon to start. You need a wall, some sticky notes, and the discipline to draw what actually happens instead of what is supposed to.

The method stack

Four toolkits, one job.

Process engineering is not one method. It is a stack of them, built by different people solving different problems, and they fit together better than their fan clubs admit. You map the work, you find the waiting, you reduce the variation, and you keep a loop running so the gains do not rot. Here is the stack, oldest idea to most operational.

Toyota · Ohno · later named by Womack & Jones
Lean has one obsession: waste. Anything the customer would not pay for if they could see it. The discipline catalogs eight kinds of it, and waiting is almost always the biggest. Value-stream mapping is the tool. You draw the whole journey and mark which steps add value and which are pure wait.
In healthcareThe waiting room is not a metaphor. It is muda. So is the chart that sits in a queue, the bed that cannot turn over, the lab that gets re-drawn because the first tube was lost.
Motorola, 1986 · scaled by GE
Where Lean hunts waste, Six Sigma hunts variation. The enemy is the process that works fine on Tuesday and fails on Friday for no reason anyone can name. Its engine is DMAIC: Define, Measure, Analyze, Improve, Control. It is data-first and it is relentless about the last letter.
In healthcareTwo patients with the same heart attack should not get care that differs by which nurse was on. Variation is where harm hides. Measuring it is half the cure.
Flowcharts · swim lanes · OMG standard
This is the notation itself, the part that is pure craft. Swim lanes, SIPOC, BPMN symbols. It sounds like the least important toolkit and it is the one most projects skip, which is why most projects fix the wrong thing. The map is the intervention. Half the value is in the drawing.
In healthcareThe first time a unit sees its own discharge drawn end to end, the room goes quiet. Nobody had ever seen the whole thing at once.
Shewhart & Deming · Goldratt's Theory of Constraints
The systems-thinking backbone. Deming gave us the feedback loop, Plan-Do-Study-Act, small tests of change run fast instead of one big rollout prayed over. Goldratt gave us the bottleneck rule: a system is only as fast as its single slowest constraint, so improving anything else is theater.
In healthcareThis is the language quality improvement already speaks. The IHI Model for Improvement is PDSA in a lab coat. Most hospitals just never connect it back to the rest of the stack.
Lean, made visible

Find the waiting.

When you map a real care episode and color the value-add time separately from the wait time, the result is always uncomfortable. The care itself, the part a clinician actually does, is a sliver. The rest of the timeline is the patient sitting, the chart queued, the order pending, the result not yet read.

One clinic visit, by the clock
Value-stream timeline
The teal slivers are the only part the patient came for. Everything else is wait. Here the care is about a tenth of the visit, and this is already a clean one. Map an episode nobody has touched and the value-add routinely falls below five percent. Lean does not start by speeding up the teal. It starts by attacking the gray, because that is where the hours are. Speed up a 22-minute exam and you save seconds. Kill an hour of waiting and you change the day.

This is the whole reason mapping comes first. Without the picture, every instinct points at the teal, because the teal is the part you can see people doing. The gray is invisible. It is the patient who is not in front of you, the chart in a queue, the result no one has opened. Lean's contribution to medicine is mostly this one reframe. Go attack the gray.

Six Sigma, made operational

Then kill the variation.

Once the waiting is under control, the next enemy is inconsistency. The classic healthcare proof is door-to-balloon time, the minutes between a heart attack patient arriving and the blocked artery being opened. Every minute is muscle. For years the time varied wildly by hospital, by shift, by luck. Then teams ran it through a loop that looks like this.

DMAIC, on door-to-balloon time
Six Sigma cycle
D
Define
The goal: artery open in under 90 minutes, every time, not on average.
M
Measure
Time-stamp every step. Door, EKG, lab call, cath team page, balloon.
A
Analyze
The delay was the page. Waiting for a cardiologist to confirm before activating.
I
Improve

C

National median door-to-balloon time fell from well over 90 minutes to under 60 across the 2000s, and the change was not a new drug or a new wire. It was a redesigned process, measured, and held. That last letter, Control, is the one everyone skips and the only reason any of it lasts.

DMAIC is not magic. It is the refusal to guess. Define what good looks like as a number. Measure the real thing, not the story about it. Find the one step that actually moves the number. Change that step. Then guard it, because every improved process drifts back toward the comfortable old one the moment you stop watching.

Deming, made into a habit

Keep the loop running.

Here is the part that separates a real engineering culture from a binder full of dead initiatives. You do not roll out one giant change and pray. You run small tests, fast, and you let what you learn from each one shape the next. Deming built it from Walter Shewhart's work and it has powered healthcare quality improvement ever since: Plan, Do, Study, Act. Then around again.

The improvement loop
Small, fast, repeated. A test you can run by Friday beats a rollout you launch next year, because the Friday test teaches you something while the big rollout is still in committee. The loop never closes. That is the point.

And running underneath all of it is Goldratt's quieter rule. A process is only as fast as its single slowest step, its constraint. The emergency department backs up not because everyone is slow but because there is one inpatient bed that will not open. Pour effort anywhere except the constraint and you get a busier system that is exactly as slow as before. Find the bottleneck. Fix the bottleneck. Then find the new one, because there is always a new one.

The payoff

This site is the map.

Now the part I have been building toward. The front page of Healthcare Uncharted makes one promise in four words: healthcare doesn't come with a map. The tagline under it is shorter still. Map the system. Understand the why. And the About page says it flattest of all: no one was mapping the full system, the clinical, financial, operational, and political layers together, so this site exists to close that gap.

Read those three lines again with the last four sections in your head. That is a process-engineering brief. You cannot close a gap you have not drawn. You cannot map a system you refuse to look at whole. The mission of this site and the first move of this discipline are the same sentence.

The honest part

Where it breaks.

If you only hear the success stories, process engineering starts to sound like a religion, and that is exactly when it gets dangerous in a hospital. So here is the other side, because the failure modes are real and most of them are on this site already, in the field guide on healthcare's laws and paradoxes.

The metric becomes the target
The moment you measure a process, people optimize the measurement, not the care. That is Goodhart's Law, and healthcare is littered with it. Hit the four-hour ED target by admitting people who should go home. Improve the readmission number by coding around it. A measured process you do not watch closely will be gamed. Count on it.

There are three more traps worth naming plainly.

The map is not the territory. A swim-lane diagram is a model, and every model leaves things out. The clean boxes hide the nurse who knows the system is wrong and quietly fixes it forty times a day. Engineer away her workaround without understanding why it exists and you can make care worse while every metric improves.

The org chart becomes the data model. That is Conway's Law. Systems end up shaped like the organizations that build them, so a fragmented health system builds fragmented workflows, and no amount of mapping fixes a structure problem you are pretending is a process problem.

The factory is not the body. Toyota's line builds identical cars. Medicine treats people who are not identical and do not want to be optimized like a transmission. Borrow the tools, yes. Import the worldview wholesale and you get the worst of both: the coldness of the assembly line and none of its reliability. The discipline serves the care. Never the other way around.

None of this is a reason to skip the work. It is a reason to do it with your eyes open. A bad map beats no map, and a humble map beats a confident one. The engineers who do real good in healthcare are the ones who hold the method loosely and the patient tightly.

Start here

What kind of broken is it?

The four toolkits are not interchangeable, and reaching for the wrong one is how improvement projects die. So before you pick a tool, name the problem. Tell me what your process actually does wrong and I will point you at the toolkit that fits, and the first move to make.

Reach for · Lean & value-stream mapping
Your problem is waiting, not working.
Slow almost never means people are too slow. It means the work sits in queues between the people. The thing you are trying to speed up is probably already fast. The hours are in the gaps.
First moveTime one real case end to end and mark every minute as work or wait. Then go attack the biggest block of wait. Ignore the work for now.
Reach for · Six Sigma & DMAIC
Same task, different result depending on who or when.
It works on Tuesday and fails on Friday. One nurse does it one way, the next does it another. That is variation, and in healthcare variation is where harm hides. You cannot fix a spread you have not measured.
First moveDefine good as a single number with a target. Then measure the actual spread across thirty real cases before you change one thing. The data will tell you where the variation enters.
Reach for · Workflow mapping & BPMN
Nobody can even say how it works.
Ask five people how the process runs and you get five answers, all of them partly wrong. There is no shared picture, so every fix lands on a different imaginary process. The map itself is the intervention here.
First moveGet the four to six people who actually do it in one room for an hour. Draw the swim lane on the wall, one lane per role. The mess shows up the moment it is all in one place.
Reach for · Theory of Constraints
Everyone is busy and nothing moves.
The whole system is flat out, and throughput still will not budge. That is the signature of a bottleneck. One step governs the pace of the entire process, and effort spent anywhere else just makes a busier system that is exactly as slow.
First moveFind the single step with a queue piling up in front of it. That is your constraint. Pour everything into that one step and nothing else, until the queue moves. Then find the next one.

4 Attack it · DMAIC charter

Define: every clean auth decided within 24 hours. Measure: timestamp submit to decision, and track the bounce rate. Analyze: the bounces trace to three missing fields, every time. Improve: a one-page completeness checklist plus a rule that nothing submits until it passes. Control: a weekly bounce-rate review so the old habit cannot creep back.

5 Test it · one PDSA

Plan: run the three-field checklist for one ordering provider, for one week. Do: applied it to the next twenty-two orders. Study: the bounce rate fell from roughly a third to near zero, and the resubmits disappeared with it. Act: adopt for that provider, then spread to the next.

The result. Nobody worked faster. They stopped doing the wait twice. Closing the rework loop, not speeding up the humans, is where the days came back. No new staff, no new software. One checklist, aimed by a map at the one crossing that was bleeding time. That is the whole discipline in a single case.
The idea, in one picture

Why it's called a bottleneck.

Forget the jargon for a second, because the word is the whole idea. Pour anything through a bottle and it does not matter how big the bottle is. Everything has to squeeze through the neck, one at a time, and the neck sets the pace. A process works exactly the same way. Somewhere in it there is a neck. The job is to find it.

Work piles up behind the narrowest point and trickles out the far side. Make the bottle bigger all you want. Until you widen the NECK, nothing moves faster.

That neck is the constraint, and it is the only thing worth fixing first. Everything poured in ahead of it just waits. Everything past it sits idle, starved for work. Relieve the neck and the whole line speeds up at once. Then, quietly, a new neck forms somewhere else, and the job begins again.

What to do Monday

Pick one process.

Do not start with a strategy. Start with one process that annoys you. The discharge, the prior auth, the morning huddle that runs forty minutes and decides nothing. Draw it on a wall with the people who actually do it. Mark the handoffs. Time the waits. You will find the problem is almost never where the complaints point, and almost always in a crossing nobody owns.

That is the entire discipline in one sitting. Map it. Find the gray. Test one small change by Friday. Watch the number. Then do it again. The rest, the certifications and the colored belts and the consultants, is commentary on those five moves.

Your first two weeks

Here is that loop, paced for a real unit instead of a whiteboard. No budget, no software, no permission you do not already have.

Day 1
Pick one process that annoys you, and name the pain as a number. Not "discharge is slow." Say "discharge takes 4 hours and we want 2."
Day 1
Get the four to six people who actually do the work in a room for sixty minutes. Not their managers. The people whose hands are on it.
Days 2–3
Draw the current state. One lane per role, sticky note every step, a mark at every handoff. Draw what happens, not what the policy says happens.
Day 4
Time one real case end to end. Mark each minute work or wait. Find the biggest single block of wait. That is your target, and it is almost never where people complain.
Day 5
Pick one change aimed at that wait, and write a one-line PDSA. Small enough to run by next Friday. If it needs a committee, it is too big.
Week 2
Run the test on one unit, one shift. Watch the number. Adopt it, adjust it, or kill it. Then aim the next cycle at whatever the new bottleneck turns out to be.
Ongoing
Hold the gain. The Control step nobody does. Audit the change until it is the new normal, or the process drifts straight back to comfortable and slow.
