Article 08 Where the system breaks

The map has holes in it.

One in five Americans lives in a rural area. Roughly one in ten physicians practices there. Every year the space between those two numbers gets a little wider, another hospital converts or closes, and another county's nearest labor and delivery unit moves an hour further away. No app has closed that gap yet.

Principle The access gap is a WORKFORCE gap wearing a geography costume. Technology can stretch clinicians. It cannot mint them, and it does not keep a hospital's doors open.
By David Eitel ~11 min read Census · HRSA · AAMC · Sheps

The geography

20% of the country, about 10% of the doctors. Federal shortage designations cover tens of millions of people, and they cluster exactly where you'd guess.

The workforce

Up to 86,000 physicians short by 2036. Nursing is strained, allied health is graying, and there is no surge-production mode for a clinician.

The hard truth

Over a hundred rural hospitals gone since 2010. Telehealth helps at the margin. Staffing is the floor, and the floor is what's cracking.

The geography

One in five, one in ten

Start with the two numbers that frame everything else. The 2020 Census puts about 20% of Americans in rural areas. The National Rural Health Association's long-standing figure puts roughly 10% of physicians there. That 2-to-1 mismatch is the gap, in its simplest form, before you touch a single other variable.

The federal government formalizes the mismatch as Health Professional Shortage Areas, HRSA designations for places short on primary care, dental, and mental health capacity. Tens of millions of Americans live inside one. Pull up the Multi-Lens Map and look at the rural and equity lenses; the shortage designations, the uninsured rates, and the non-expansion states stack on the same counties over and over. The holes cluster. That is the whole point of mapping them.

And it compounds. A county that loses its hospital gets harder to recruit to, which thins the workforce further, which makes the next closure more likely. Nobody designed that loop. It runs anyway.

The workforce

You cannot surge-produce a clinician

The AAMC projects a physician shortfall of up to 86,000 by 2036. Read the fine print and it gets worse: the projection is national, and shortage never distributes evenly. It pools where recruiting is hardest, which is the same map from the last section.

Nursing is not a backstop. The national workforce surveys keep saying the same thing in politer language: burnout and staffing strain persist, and the experience is walking out the door faster than the pipeline replaces it. And the staffing math has a body count. Aiken showed it two decades ago: each additional patient loaded onto a nurse raised the odds of patient mortality and of nurse burnout. Understaffing is not an HR metric. It is a clinical outcome.

I came up through respiratory therapy, so here is the allied health version: the pipeline problems are the same, the visibility is worse, and whole service lines quietly depend on professions most administrators could not describe. Look at the Career Tree colored by BLS growth. The demand is right there in federal data. The people are not.

Here is the constraint nobody can budget away: it takes about a decade to make a physician, years to make a nurse or a therapist, and the training capacity itself, residency slots, clinical sites, faculty, is part of what's short. When the input pipeline takes ten years, the 2036 shortfall is already baked. We are arguing about the size of it, not the existence of it.

Understaffing is not an HR metric. It is a clinical outcome with a literature behind it.
The closures

What leaves first

The Sheps Center at UNC keeps the closure ledger, and it is grim reading: more than a hundred rural hospitals have closed outright since 2010, with dozens more converting away from inpatient care. Each one of those is a county where "go to the hospital" became a road trip.

Closure has an order of operations. Labor and delivery goes first, because OB is expensive to staff and brutal to insure. Behavioral health beds follow. Then the service lines thin until what is left is an ED, a lab, and a prayer, and then one Friday the whole thing is a press release. The federal Critical Access Hospital designation exists precisely because standard payment math kills small rural hospitals; whole stretches of the interior run on that carve-out. See it yourself: the Operators Map, critical access view. That preset is one click because this pattern deserves one click.

And do not file closures under "rural charm fading." When a hospital system fails, the machinery is finance, and the casualties are staff and patients. We did the autopsy on one: the Steward post-mortem. The mechanics travel.

LessonA closure is never one bad year. It is payment math, staffing, and distance compounding until the doors close, in that order, with OB gone long before the press release.
The tech question

Multiplication by zero

So can technology close the gap? Telehealth earns its keep at the margin, and I say that as someone who builds on the clinical informatics side for a living. It removes the windshield time for a follow-up visit, extends specialists into places that will never recruit one, and got a decade of regulatory slack in three pandemic years. The state-by-state law tracker is worth a bookmark, because the rules are still settling.

But walk the dependency chain. A telehealth visit requires broadband, a device, a licensed clinician on the other end with capacity to spare, and someone local when the encounter turns physical. Three of those four are exactly the things shortage areas are short on. Technology multiplies clinicians. Multiplication by zero is still zero.

Same logic for AI. Ambient scribes hand clinicians back minutes, and that is real; I wrote a whole article about what AI actually delivers. But minutes returned to an existing clinician do not staff an empty county. And if the efficiency gain just gets absorbed as more volume for the same workforce, that is Jevons, on schedule, wearing scrubs.

What moves it

The levers that actually reach

The honest list is short and unglamorous, and every item on it is policy, not product.

Train more, closer: residency slots and clinical training capacity in the places that need the graduates, because clinicians disproportionately stay where they train. Let people work: scope-of-practice and licensure compacts decide whether the clinicians who already exist can practice where the shortage is, which is why the fights over them are so loud. I spend real hours of my life in that arena on the respiratory side, and I can tell you the map on the scope-of-practice tile is not decoration. And pay rural differently: the Critical Access designation was the admission, back in 1997, that standard reimbursement math structurally fails small hospitals. The question was never whether payment bends for geography. It already does. The question is whether it bends enough.

Notice what is NOT on the list: an app. Not because the tools are useless, but because every one of them multiplies a workforce that has to exist first.

The gap is not a mystery. It is a bill. Somebody pays it in dollars, or the people living in the holes keep paying it in miles.
