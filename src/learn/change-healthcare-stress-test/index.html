Article 09 System stress test

The stress test nobody ordered.

On February 12, 2024, someone logged into a Change Healthcare remote-access portal with stolen credentials. The portal did not have multi-factor authentication. Nine days later they encrypted the company from the inside, and American healthcare found out exactly how much of itself ran through one vendor. All of this is on the Congressional record.

Principle Every system gets stress-tested eventually. The only choice is whether you run the test or your single point of failure runs it for you. Healthcare got the second kind, and the results are worth reading like the lab report they are.
By David Eitel ~11 min read SEC · Senate Finance · HHS OCR · Treasury OFR
Cybersecurity Revenue Cycle Vendors Commercial Insurance
The breach

192.7 million people. The largest healthcare data breach in American history started with one set of compromised credentials on a portal without MFA, and ended with a $22 million ransom paid in Bitcoin.

The dependency

15 billion transactions a year. One in three patient records. $2 trillion in claims. Treasury's own analysts called it what it was: a single point of failure with exclusivity clauses.

The lesson

Resilience is a structure problem. The rule that would mandate the missing MFA was proposed in January 2025. It still isn't final. Map your own dependencies; nobody upstream is doing it for you.

Nine days in February

One login, no second factor

The mechanics are almost insultingly simple. This was not a zero-day, not nation-state tradecraft, not some exotic supply-chain implant. UnitedHealth Group's CEO told the Senate Finance Committee exactly what happened: criminals used compromised credentials to log into a Citrix remote-access portal, and the portal did not have multi-factor authentication. The control that your bank requires for a $50 transfer was not protecting the front door of the company that moves a third of America's patient records.

Feb 12
Entry. Stolen credentials open the Citrix portal. No MFA challenge. The intruders move laterally and start exfiltrating data.
Feb 21
Detonation. ALPHV/BlackCat ransomware encrypts Change Healthcare's systems. UHG isolates the environment and files its 8-K the next day. Pharmacies nationwide lose electronic prescribing and claims routing.
Feb 27
Context arrives. The FBI/CISA/HHS advisory notes that since December, healthcare has been ALPHV's most-victimized sector, after the group's administrator urged affiliates to target hospitals.
March
The ransom. UHG pays $22 million in Bitcoin. Witty, under oath: "the decision to pay a ransom was mine." Pharmacy services return by March 7, payments March 15, claims testing March 18.
After
The count climbs. The breach notification starts at a placeholder 500 people in July 2024. Then 100 million in October. Then 190 million in January 2025. The current official figure: 192.7 million individuals.

The dependency

How much fits through one company

Most clinicians had never heard of Change Healthcare on February 20. Here is what the people who measure these things knew. Fifteen billion healthcare transactions a year. One in every three American patient records. Roughly 40% of all medical claims, by the House committee's count. Treasury's Office of Financial Research put the dollar figure at $2 trillion in annual claims, about 44% of the money that would move through the U.S. medical system that year.

The concentration was not an accident, and it was not a secret. The Department of Justice sued to block UnitedHealth's $13 billion acquisition of Change in 2022, calling the company "a critical data highway through which about half of all Americans' health insurance claims pass each year." A federal court sided with UnitedHealth. The deal closed in October 2022. Sixteen months later the highway closed too, and the market-definition argument the government lost in court got settled empirically, on every practice's cash-flow statement, in about a week.

Treasury's postmortem found the structural rot under the concentration: Change held exclusivity clauses with more than a third of its clients, and some payers had NO backup payment system at all. Not a degraded one. None.

The two weeks the money stopped

Payroll from personal savings

The AHA surveyed roughly a thousand hospitals three weeks in: 94% reported financial impact, over 80% had cash flow disrupted, and of those, nearly 60% were losing a million dollars or more in revenue PER DAY. Almost three quarters reported effects on direct patient care. For the hospitals, it was a crisis with a treasury department attached.

For small practices it was closer to a bank run. In the AMA's survey of mostly sub-ten-physician practices: 80% lost revenue to unpaid claims, 55% of owners dipped into PERSONAL funds to cover practice expenses, 31% could not make payroll, 44% could not buy supplies. Sit with the 55% for a second. Physicians paying their staff out of family savings because a vendor three layers removed from their exam rooms skipped a login control.

The scale of the emergency financing tells you the rest. UnitedHealth ultimately advanced $9.03 billion in loans to providers during 2024, per its own audited cash-flow statement, less than half repaid by year-end. CMS stood up an advance-payment program that moved another $2.55 billion to hospitals and $717 million to Part B suppliers. One clearinghouse went down and the response required more emergency liquidity than most natural disasters.

And the final bill, from UnitedHealth's own earnings tables: $3.09 billion in full-year cyberattack costs, $2.2 billion of it direct response and the rest business disruption. For sector context, IBM's annual study put the AVERAGE healthcare breach at $9.77 million that year, the costliest of any industry for the fourteenth year running. This one cost three hundred times the average, before you count anything that happened outside UnitedHealth's walls.

The lab report

What the test proved

Read it as the stress test it was, and the findings write themselves. Finding one: the failure mode was boring. Credential hygiene and MFA, the same two controls on every security checklist since the 2010s. Finding two: the blast radius was structural, not technical. The damage scaled with market concentration, exclusivity clauses, and absent backups, none of which any hospital's IT department could see or fix. Finding three: the system had no circuit breakers. The money simply stopped until one company's engineers rebuilt, and the federal response was cash, not architecture.

So what did the system DO about it? The scoreboard, two and a half years on. HHS published voluntary cybersecurity performance goals in January 2024. The Wyden-Warner bill that would have made standards mandatory, with real audits and money for safety-net hospitals, died with the 118th Congress without a vote. And the one regulatory vehicle with teeth, OCR's proposed overhaul of the HIPAA Security Rule, which would mandate MFA, encryption, and asset inventories and cites the Change breach throughout its preamble, was published in January 2025, closed comments in March 2025, and as of this writing is still not final.

The control whose absence cost $3 billion, 192.7 million records, and a month of national cash flow is, today, still legally optional.

LessonNobody upstream is running your dependency analysis. The stress test found the structure brittle, and the structure has mostly not changed. UnitedHealth's revenue the year after the attack: $400 billion.
Do this

Your own dependency map

If you lead anything that bills, ask three questions this quarter. Which clearinghouse and which intermediaries does our revenue actually flow through, and what percentage rides each one? What is the backup route, and has anyone ever exercised it, not diagrammed it, EXERCISED it? And how many days of operating cash survive a full stop in claims payments? The practices that answered those questions before February 2024 had a bad month. The ones that couldn't had an existential one.

Then put the MFA question in writing to every vendor holding your data or your money. Not "do you have a security program." Specifically: is multi-factor authentication enforced on every remote access path, no exceptions, and will you attest to that annually? It is a one-sentence email. The entire event you just read about happens if one company answers it honestly.
