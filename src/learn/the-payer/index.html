Article 04 The payer

The payer reads a different chart.

You see a patient. The insurer sees an 837 file: every encounter translated into codes, run through adjudication rules, and answered with a payment, a denial, or a request for more paperwork. That translation layer is a parallel healthcare system, and it decides what the visible one gets paid for.

Principle In the payer's world, care only exists once it is coded, and every code is a negotiation. Learn to read the pipeline and the denial statistics stop looking like verdicts. They start looking like what they are: a process, with a documented overturn rate.
By David Eitel ~12 min read KFF · CMS · AMA · HHS OIG

The pipeline

HIPAA mandates the rails. X12 claims in, remittances out. In 2024, HealthCare.gov insurers denied 19% of in-network claims. Fewer than 1% were ever appealed.

The lens

~74,000 diagnosis codes, 11,000 CPT codes, 767 DRGs. The payer's entire view of medicine is these code sets, plus math on top. Coding intensity alone is worth $40 billion a year in Medicare Advantage.

The gap

When people DO appeal, they win. 80.7% of appealed Medicare Advantage denials were overturned in 2024, above 80% every year since 2019. The OIG found 13% of denials met coverage rules all along.

The pipeline

The parallel system

Every claim in the country rides the same rails. HIPAA's administrative simplification rules mandate the X12 transaction formats: the 837 carries the claim, the 835 carries the payment and the explanation, the 270 and 271 handle eligibility. This standardization is one of the quiet wins of American health policy. CAQH's industry index credits automation of these transactions with $258 billion in avoided spending, with about $21 billion a year still on the table for the workflows that haven't finished converting.

Then the claim hits adjudication, and the win rate changes hands. In 2024, insurers on HealthCare.gov denied 19% of in-network claims, roughly 85 million of 451 million. Denial rates by insurer ranged from 3% to 36%, same law, same marketplace. And here is the number that should reframe every conversation about denials: the stated reason was "medical necessity" only 5% of the time. The biggest reported buckets were "other" at 36% and administrative at 25%. Most denials are not a clinical judgment. They are the pipeline rejecting paperwork.

The system's asymmetry lives in that number: one side runs the process at industrial scale, the other side mostly never engages with it. Keep that asymmetry in mind. It comes back with teeth in the prior auth section.

The lens

The codes ARE the chart

The payer never meets the patient. Its entire epistemology is a stack of code sets, each owned by someone, each updated on its own calendar, each carrying money.

ICD-10-CM
The diagnosis. Roughly 74,000 codes maintained by CDC/NCHS, updated every October. This is what was WRONG with the patient, as far as the payer knows.
CPT
The work. More than 11,000 codes describing what was done, owned and licensed by the AMA. The 2026 set added 288 new ones, heavy on emerging tech.
HCPCS II
The stuff. CMS-maintained codes for equipment, drugs, ambulances, everything that isn't professional work.
MS-DRG
The hospital stay, flattened. Medicare pays inpatient care as one predetermined rate per discharge, assigned to one of 767 diagnosis-related groups. The whole admission becomes a single number.
RVU
The physician's worth, in units. Work, practice expense, and malpractice values times a conversion factor: $32.35 in 2025, splitting to $33.40 or $33.57 in 2026 depending on payment-model participation. Yes, the entire physician fee schedule hangs off one multiplier.
HCC
The risk score. In Medicare Advantage, submitted diagnoses feed a risk model that scales the plan's monthly payment. Which means in MA, a diagnosis code is not a clinical fact. It is revenue.

How much is coding worth? MedPAC estimates Medicare pays MA plans about 20% more than traditional Medicare for comparable beneficiaries, $84 billion in 2025 alone, and attributes $40 billion of it to coding intensity: the same patients, documented harder. When a code set carries that kind of money, the documentation stops being a record of care and becomes an instrument of payment. Both sides know it. Only one side staffs it accordingly.

In this system, documentation is not paperwork about the care. Documentation IS the care, as far as anyone paying can see.
The gate

Prior authorization, measured

Prior authorization is where the parallel system reaches forward in time: approval BEFORE care instead of payment after it. The AMA's 2025 physician survey puts the operational load in plain numbers. Practices complete 40 prior auths per physician per week and spend 13 hours a week doing it. Forty percent of physicians employ staff whose entire job is prior auth.

The clinical numbers are worse than the operational ones. 95% of physicians report PA delays access to necessary care. 26% report a prior auth led to a SERIOUS adverse event for a patient in their care, and within that group, 8% report a disability, birth defect, or death. 79% report patients abandoning treatment at least sometimes because of the process. And 88% report the thing the actuaries should care about: prior auth often drives overall utilization UP, through failed step therapy, extra visits, and the ER trips that happen while an approval is pending.

Now the shape of the machine, from KFF's Medicare Advantage data. 52.8 million PA determinations in 2024, about 1.7 per enrollee. Traditional Medicare, covering a comparable population, ran about 625,000 reviews, two per HUNDRED beneficiaries. Of the MA denials, 11.5% were appealed. Of those appeals, 80.7% were overturned, and the overturn rate has topped 80% every single year since 2019. The HHS Inspector General, auditing the largest MA insurers, found 13% of denied PA requests met Medicare coverage rules at the moment of denial.

Put those numbers in one sentence: a denial that is appealed usually dies, and most denials are never appealed. Whatever that process is optimized for, it is not accuracy.

LessonThe gap between the denial rate and the overturn rate is the entire argument. Appeal. The published numbers say the first answer is frequently not the real answer.
The referee's math

Floors, rebates, and concentration

The main federal control on insurer economics is the medical loss ratio: at least 80 cents of every premium dollar (85 in large group, and 85 for Medicare Advantage and Medicaid managed care) must go to claims and quality improvement, or the difference comes back as rebates. Real money moves: $1.6 billion rebated in 2025, $14.4 billion cumulative since 2012. Notice what an MLR floor does NOT constrain: the absolute size of the pie. Fifteen percent of a bigger number is a bigger number.

And the pie is consolidating. The AMA's market study finds 97% of metropolitan commercial insurance markets highly concentrated. In 47% of them, a single insurer holds at least half the market. Nationally, UnitedHealth holds 16% of commercial enrollment, with Elevance and CVS/Aetna at 12% each. Medicare Advantage now covers 55% of eligible beneficiaries, and UnitedHealth plus Humana hold 46% of it. One layer down, the FTC reports the three largest pharmacy benefit managers, each owned by a company that also owns a top insurer, processed nearly 80% of America's 6.6 billion prescriptions.

This is the context for every negotiation story you have ever heard from a practice manager. The pipeline is standardized, the codes are public, and the counterparty across the table keeps getting bigger. If you want to see what happens when this much of the money layer runs through one company, February 2024 already ran the experiment.

The counted era

The pipeline gets a clock

Two forces are currently rewiring this system, one regulatory and one reputational. The regulatory one is CMS's Interoperability and Prior Authorization rule: starting in 2026, affected payers owe PA decisions within 72 hours for urgent requests and 7 days for standard ones, must give specific denial reasons, and must publish their PA metrics. By 2027 the whole exchange moves to FHIR APIs, prior auth included. CMS prices the efficiency gain around $15 billion over ten years.

The reputational one arrived in June 2025, when insurers covering 257 million Americans signed a voluntary pledge to shrink PA volume, honor existing authorizations across plan switches, answer most electronic requests in real time by 2027, and put medical professionals on every clinical denial. Worth watching. Worth skepticism too: in the AMA's survey, exactly 33% of physicians believe the pledge will make a meaningful difference. The profession has seen voluntary before.

LessonDeadlines, published metrics, and machine-readable denials mean the pipeline is about to become MEASURABLE from the outside. Measured systems behave differently. That is rather the point.
Do this

Working a system that counts

If you practice: appeal, and build the habit into the workflow rather than the exception. The overturn statistics are not an anecdote, they are six consecutive years of federal data. If you run a practice: know your own denial distribution the way the payer knows it, because "other" and "administrative" are fixable categories, and the cost of NOT working a denial is documented too. If you're the informaticist: the 2026-2027 FHIR deadlines mean the PA machinery is about to live in your integration backlog. Get ahead of it.

And whichever seat you sit in, remember what the payer can and cannot see. It cannot see the patient. It can only see what the documentation became after translation. That is not a reason to game the codes. It is a reason to make the codes tell the truth at full resolution, because in this system, an undocumented severity is clinically real and financially fictional at the same time.
