Rounds 03 · Workforce · Mental Health

The Wound and the Workload

TL;DR

We called it burnout, aimed the fix at the worker, and told them to be more resilient. Five years of data say it was the workplace all along.

  • Physician burnout spiked to 62.8% at the pandemic peak in 2021 and has fallen every year since, down to 41.9% in 2025, near where it sat before COVID. That line is recovering.
  • The nurse line is not. RN turnover ticked back UP in 2025 to 17.6% after three years of decline. More than 138,000 nurses have left since 2022, and four in ten say they plan to leave within five years. The two front-line groups are on different trajectories.
  • The fix that works is the one nobody wants to pay for. Two separate meta-analyses found that changing the system beats changing the worker by about two and a half to one. And 29% of the most resilient physicians were burned out anyway. Resilience was never the missing ingredient.

Briefing

The word we chose, and the wound under it

Dr. Lorna Breen ran the emergency department at NewYork-Presbyterian Allen. In the first COVID wave she worked through patients dying in hallways, caught the virus herself, went back to the job before she had recovered, and in April 2020 she died by suicide. Her family says she had no history of mental illness, and that she was afraid seeking help would cost her license. Her father said the line everyone remembers afterward. She tried to do her job, and it killed her (Dr. Lorna Breen Heroes' Foundation, 2020).

What came after, we called burnout. And the word matters more than it looks.

Burnout points at the person. It says the worker ran out of something. Resilience, grit, balance, whatever. So the fix that follows always aims the same direction, at the individual. Take the resilience course. Download the meditation app. Come to the pizza party in the break room. Be a little tougher next shift.

Two physicians, Wendy Dean and Simon Talbot, said the word was wrong back in 2018. What clinicians were describing was not burnout, they argued. It was moral injury, a term borrowed from combat, the damage done when you are stopped from doing what you know is right for the person in front of you (Talbot & Dean, 2018). The distinction is not academic. Burnout asks what is wrong with you. Moral injury asks what is wrong with the place.

So here is five years of data, from the COVID spike to now. Two threads run through all of it. The wound, meaning what the work did to people's minds. And the workload, meaning the staffing math that caused a lot of the wound. They are not separate stories. They feed each other.

Assessment

Five years, three readings

Reading 1 · The curve that bent, and the one that didn't · AMA, 2026 & NSI, 2026

Start with the good news, because it is real. Physician burnout peaked in 2021, the Omicron winter, when 62.8% of doctors reported at least one symptom. By 2025 it was down to 41.9%, four straight years of decline, back near the pre-pandemic mark (AMA, 2026). Doctors are climbing out. Now the other front-line group. Nurses do not get measured the same clean way, there is no national burnout percentage tracked year over year the way there is for physicians, so the proxy is turnover, and turnover tells a worse story. RN turnover hit 27.1% in 2021, came down for three years, and then in 2025 it turned around and went back UP to 17.6% (NSI, 2026). One line is healing. The other just reopened. Same buildings, same pandemic, two different recoveries, and the difference is worth sitting with.

Reading 2 · The wound · CDC, 2023; Davis et al., 2021; Makhija et al., 2025

The federal numbers are blunt. In the CDC's worker survey, health workers reporting burnout often or very often went from 32% in 2018 to 46% in 2022. Harassment on the job doubled, 6% to 13%. By 2022, 57% said they had felt anxious (CDC, 2023). During the worst of COVID, roughly one in five health workers screened positive for depression, anxiety, and PTSD, about five times the rate in the general population (Li et al., 2021). And then the hardest edge of it. Suicide. Female nurses die by suicide at about twice the rate of women in the general population. Female physicians at about one and a half times (Davis et al., 2021; Makhija et al., 2025). Say the next part carefully, because the easy version is wrong: male physicians are NOT at elevated risk, they sit at or below average. The danger concentrates among women in these fields, and a slogan that flattens that is a slogan that helps no one. This is the wound. It is not a metaphor.

Reading 3 · The workload · Aiken et al., 2002; HRSA, 2024; AAMC, 2024

Now the cause hiding under the wound. Linda Aiken's foundational work put a number on it more than two decades ago: each additional patient added to a nurse's load is associated with a 7% jump in patient mortality and a 23% jump in the odds of nurse burnout (Aiken et al., 2002). Understaffing is not a symptom of burnout. It is a driver of it. Which sets up the loop that has run since 2020. Burnout pushes people out, more than 138,000 nurses gone since 2022, four in ten of the rest planning to leave within five years (NCSBN, 2025). Each exit thins the staffing further, which loads the people who stay, which burns them out, which pushes more of them out. The demographics make it worse, not better. HRSA projects the country will be short about 338,000 registered nurses by 2036. The AAMC projects up to 86,000 physicians (HRSA, 2024; AAMC, 2024). The travel-nurse market told us this in dollars years ago, when agency rates ran up 213% between early 2019 and early 2022. That was not a labor shortage. That was a building on fire and hospitals bidding for the last bucket.

At the Bedside

You cannot meditate your way out of being short three nurses

Here is the part that should end the debate, and somehow has not. When researchers compared the two ways of attacking burnout, the ones aimed at the organization, cutting workload, fixing schedules, reducing clerical load, beat the ones aimed at the individual by roughly two and a half to one. Two separate meta-analyses, six years apart, landed on nearly the identical number (Panagioti et al., 2017; Zaçe et al., 2023). And the kicker that should retire the resilience industry for good: physicians as a group are MORE resilient than the general working public, and even among the most resilient of them, 29% were burned out anyway (West et al., 2020). You cannot out-resilience a broken schedule.

So what is the workload actually made of. A lot of it is invisible work. Clinicians get hired at one full-time job and quietly work about 1.2 of them, the extra fifth made of after-hours admin nobody counts or pays for (NAM, 2026). The electronic record is a big chunk of it. Physicians spend about two hours on the EHR and desk work for every one hour of face time with a patient, then another 86 minutes at night, the thing clinicians grimly call pajama time (Sinsky et al., 2016; Arndt et al., 2017). The systems they are forced to use score an F on standard usability tests (Melnick et al., 2020). This is not softness. This is a job that has had unpaid, unglamorous, infuriating work bolted onto it for fifteen years.

And now watch what gets sold as the cure. Ambient AI scribes, the tools that listen to the visit and write the note, are being pitched hard as the burnout fix. Some early data looks encouraging, one study saw burnout drop from 52% to 39% after a month on the tool. But the cleanest test we have, a randomized trial, found no significant improvement in burnout, and clinicians rated the AI notes as worse than the ones they write themselves. Reviewers have flagged that these tools can fabricate exam findings that never happened (Lukac et al., 2025; Topaz et al., 2025). Set that next to what the last Rounds piece found, that the measured financial return on those same scribes was MORE billing. So the tool being sold to heal the wound may turn out to be very good at optimizing the bill, and only pretty good at the wound. We have seen this pattern before. The technology arrives as a gift to the worker and settles in as a gift to whoever owns the revenue.

Follow-Up

Four things worth tracking

  1. Whether the nurse line follows the doctors, or keeps diverging. The physician recovery is real and measured. The nurse turnover uptick in 2025 is the early warning that their crisis is not following the same arc (NSI, 2026). If that number climbs again in the 2027 report, the two-trajectory story hardens into the headline.
  2. Whether safe-staffing laws spread, and whether they actually move burnout. California has mandated nurse-to-patient ratios since 2004, Oregon since 2023, and a federal ratio bill is sitting in committee. Ratios are the single most direct lever on the workload half of this. The test is not whether more states pass them. It is whether the places that do show lower burnout and better outcomes, which the existing evidence says they should (Aiken et al., 2002).
  3. Whether ambient AI cuts burnout in controlled trials, not productivity reports. The pitch is wellbeing. The evidence so far is mostly billing and self-selected surveys. Watch for randomized, controlled results measured on the clinician, not on the RVU. Until those land, treat the burnout claim as marketing.
  4. Where the Lorna Breen money lands. The Dr. Lorna Breen Act was reauthorized through 2030. It is the rare federal effort aimed at the system rather than the individual. The question is whether the funding builds structural change, better staffing, lighter documentation, real mental-health access without career punishment, or whether it quietly buys another round of resilience webinars. Follow the dollars, not the press release.

Leading indicators worth tracking

  • The next NSI and NCSBN nurse turnover and intent-to-leave figures, as the clearest read on whether the workload loop is still spinning
  • State safe-staffing legislation, and any outcome data from California and Oregon's existing ratios
  • Randomized trials of ambient AI documentation measured on burnout and note quality, not time saved or RVUs
  • Licensing boards that remove intrusive mental-health questions, the structural barrier that kept Lorna Breen from seeking help
  • Hospital staffing ratios and contract-labor spend as a proxy for how thin the floors actually are
  • Whether organizational interventions, not wellness programs, show up in health-system budgets
Wider View

Not soft. Structural.

This was never a story about weak clinicians. The data says the opposite. The people being told to toughen up are measurably tougher than the people telling them, and they broke anyway. When a workplace reliably breaks its strongest people, that is not a character problem in the people. That is a design problem in the workplace.

The fix is known and unglamorous. Staff the units. Cut the documentation load. Give people enough time to do the work right. Stop pretending a meditation app is a substitute for a third nurse on the floor. None of that fits on a wellness flyer, and all of it costs money the system would rather spend somewhere with a better return. So the system keeps reaching for the cheap version, the one that asks the worker to absorb the damage, because asking people to endure has always been cheaper than fixing the thing doing the damage.

The wound and the workload are the same story told twice. One in the language of depression and suicide, the other in the language of turnover and vacancy rates. Both trace back to the same root, and both get answered with the same dodge, which is to make it the worker's job to survive a system nobody will pay to repair.

Resilience was never the thing in short supply.
