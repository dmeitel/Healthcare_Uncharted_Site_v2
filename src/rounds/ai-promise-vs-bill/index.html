Rounds 02 · Health AI · Health Costs

The Promise and the Bill

TL;DR

AI was sold as the cure for health care's cost problem. Medicare's own billing data tells a different story.

  • Medicare claims for AI-enabled care grew from 4,135 in 2018 to 157,956 in 2023, a 3,720% jump, with beneficiaries rising nearly as fast. AI did not show up as a discount. It showed up as a new, fast-growing billable line.
  • The rigorous evidence that AI actually saves money is thin and narrow. A 2025 systematic review found only 19 economic evaluations, mostly in screening, and warned the reported savings are likely overstated.
  • Where AI's return gets measured at the bedside, it shows up as more billing, not less. Ambient documentation tools raised physician productivity by about $3,044 a year each. The value AI captures best is the value that gets coded.

Briefing

The pitch, and the receipts

For about a decade now, the case for artificial intelligence (AI) in medicine has carried a cost promise tucked inside it. Fewer unnecessary tests. Earlier catches that head off expensive disease. Less waste, less administrative drag, fewer people doing work a model could do for pennies. Bend the curve, the pitch goes. Every vendor deck has a version of it, and most of them put a dollar sign on the slide.

The receipts are now in, at least for Medicare. The Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services (CMS) started paying for AI-enabled care in 2018, beginning with a handful of coronary artery disease codes that June. A November 2025 analysis from Manatt Health pulled the claims data for the five years that followed (Augenstein et al., 2025). The annual volume of Medicare claims and encounters for AI-enabled care went from 4,135 in 2018 to 157,956 in 2023. That is a 3,720% increase. The number of beneficiaries receiving that care climbed from 2,582 to 104,769 over the same window, a 3,958% increase. Roughly forty times more, in five years.

Set that against the backdrop it lives in. National health spending hit $5.3 trillion in 2024, about 18% of the entire U.S. economy and up 7.2% in a single year, now closed in the final accounts rather than forecast (CMS Office of the Actuary, 2026). CMS actuaries project it reaching $8.6 trillion and 20.3% of gross domestic product (GDP) by 2033 (CMS Office of the Actuary, 2025). The cost curve AI was supposed to bend is still climbing. And the AI billing line is climbing inside it, not against it.

Assessment

Three readings of the same data

Reading 1 · The billing curve · Augenstein et al., 2025

The Manatt analysis is the cleanest look we have at AI hitting the ledger, because Medicare pays by code and codes leave a trail. From 4,135 claims to 157,956 in five years is not adoption noise. It is a category being born. The leading use case was coronary artery disease evaluation, the Current Procedural Terminology (CPT) codes 0501T through 0504T, which alone accounted for roughly 100,000 of those recipients, followed by diabetic retinopathy screening, coronary atherosclerosis analysis, liver imaging, and electrocardiogram-based cardiac dysfunction detection. Worth saying plainly: a billing code is not proof of waste. Some of this care is genuinely good. But a billing code is proof of a bill. Every one of those 157,956 claims is a payment that did not exist before, for a service the same patient population was, by and large, already being cared for without.

Reading 2 · The thin evidence base · El Arab & Al Moosa, 2025

So does it pay for itself? Here is where the story gets uncomfortable for the pitch. A systematic review published in npj Digital Medicine in August 2025 went looking for the economic evidence on clinical AI, screened 312 records, and found nineteen studies rigorous enough to include (El Arab & Al Moosa, 2025). Nineteen. Across oncology, cardiology, ophthalmology, and infectious disease, mostly in screening contexts, spread across three continents. The headline read well: AI improved diagnostic accuracy, added quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), and reduced costs by cutting unnecessary procedures. Then the authors did the honest thing and listed what the studies left out. Many leaned on static models that may overestimate benefit. Indirect costs, infrastructure investment, and equity were routinely underreported. Their own conclusion: the reported economic benefits "may be overstated." We are deploying AI across the system at the scale of a hundred thousand Medicare beneficiaries a year, and the careful cost-effectiveness literature underneath that is nineteen studies deep, and it is hedging.

Reading 3 · What "return" actually measures · Holmgren et al., 2026 & CMS OACT, 2025

Look at where AI's return on investment is being demonstrated most cleanly, and notice what it is measuring. A January 2026 study in JAMA Network Open tracked physicians using ambient AI scribes, the tools that listen to a visit and draft the note (Holmgren et al., 2026). Adopters generated 1.81 more relative value units (RVUs) per week than non-adopters, which the authors translate to about $3,044 per physician per year on the 2025 Physician Fee Schedule (PFS). That is the win. Read it again. The clearest financial case for clinical AI so far is that it helps a doctor bill more. More RVUs, more encounters, the same denial rate. In a fee-for-service (FFS) system, more billing is more cost to whoever is paying, which is the payer, which is ultimately the premium and the taxpayer. AI is very good at capturing value. The question nobody on the vendor slide answers is who that captured value flows to, and the honest answer, on this evidence, is not the patient and not the person at the bedside.

At the Bedside

We have seen this movie

If the shape of this feels familiar, it should. The last technology American medicine adopted at national scale on a cost-savings promise was the electronic health record (EHR). The pitch in 2009 was nearly identical: digitize the record, cut waste, coordinate care, save billions. What clinicians actually got was a documentation burden that became a leading driver of burnout, and a tool that turned out to be exceptionally good at one thing in particular, capturing charges. The EHR did not lower the bill. It optimized it. Coding got more complete, more visits hit higher levels, and the technology paid for itself by helping the system bill at the top of its range.

AI is walking the same path, faster. The ambient scribe is the cleanest example, a tool clinicians genuinely like, that genuinely lifts a real burden, and whose measured financial return is more billing. That is not a knock on the clinicians using it or the patients getting better notes. It is a description of what a fee-for-service system rewards. Drop a powerful new capability into a payment model that pays per service, and the capability will find its way to more services. The model does what the incentives ask of it.

From the clinical informatics seat, the part that does not fit on a dashboard is the same part that mattered with the EHR. Who carries the new work, who captures the new value, and whether the patient at the center of it ever sees a dollar of the savings they were promised. So far the honest answer is that the value lands with whoever owns the billing.

Follow-Up

Four things worth tracking

  1. Net savings, not productivity. The open question is whether any large clinical AI deployment can show net system savings, money the system spent less of overall, rather than productivity gains that translate into more billing. The npj review found promising but caveated cost-effectiveness in narrow screening settings (El Arab & Al Moosa, 2025). The test is whether that holds up in real deployments at scale, measured on the total bill, not the per-physician RVU.
  2. The code set is the tell. AI billing volume tracks the CPT codes CMS chooses to cover. The line jumped because coronary artery disease codes opened in 2018 (Augenstein et al., 2025). As CMS adds AI codes, watch the billing follow. The reimbursement decision, not the technology, is the lever that moves spend. And the people pulling that lever are still working out the pricing. In the 2026 Physician Fee Schedule, CMS received cost data on AI and software tools, declined to use it over quality concerns, and asked the public how it should even value the work of a clinician interpreting an AI output (CMS, 2025). The codes are running ahead of anyone's ability to price them.
  3. A 95-fold geographic gap. In 2023, AI-enabled care ranged from 136 recipients per million beneficiaries in Vermont to nearly 13,000 per million in Minnesota (Augenstein et al., 2025). That is not a small regional wrinkle. It is either a massive access gap or a massive overuse signal, and which one it is matters enormously. Worth watching whether the spread narrows or hardens.
  4. Who keeps the savings, if there are any. If clinical AI does eventually bend a cost curve, the policy question is who pockets the difference. The payer, the health system, the vendor, or the patient through a lower premium. On current structure, the patient is last in that line. Any serious AI cost story has to answer this one out loud.

Leading indicators worth tracking

  • New AI-specific CPT and reimbursement codes approved by CMS, and the billing volume that follows each one
  • Peer-reviewed evaluations that measure net system cost, not per-clinician productivity or RVUs
  • Denial rates on AI-enabled claims, an early signal of whether payers push back on the new line
  • Whether ambient documentation tools are tied to higher coding levels over time, not just more notes
  • State-level utilization spread, as a marker of access gaps versus overuse
  • FDA clearance volume for AI devices versus the share that ever earns a reimbursement pathway
Update · June 17, 2026

Two weeks later, the payers said it out loud

This posted on June 3. On June 11, the other side of the table confirmed it. PwC's annual medical cost trend report surveyed health plan actuaries and asked what was pushing spending up next year. Nearly 70% of them named providers' AI documentation and coding tools as a top-three cost driver. About one in five called AI the single biggest inflationary trend, full stop (PwC, 2026).

Look at the mechanism they describe, because it is the exact one in this piece. More complete notes let clinicians record more diagnoses, code for more complex care, and collect higher reimbursement, even when the treatment itself does not change. That is not the payers describing better medicine. That is the payers describing better billing. And they are now buying software to catch it, which is a brand new cost stacked on top of the first one.

The plans put commercial cost growth at 9% next year, the steepest in nearly two decades. AI is not the only reason. But this is the first time the people who actually write the checks have named it as one, in their own numbers, with nobody editorializing on their behalf. The argument here was that AI learns to bill before it learns to save. The payers just filed the receipt.

Wider View

Not anti-AI. Pro-honesty about where the value lands.

None of this says the technology is bad. Some of these tools catch disease earlier, read scans the eye misses, and pull real drudgery off clinicians who have too much of it. That is worth something. The argument is narrower and harder to wave away: the cost-savings promise is being made far ahead of the cost-savings evidence, and in a payment system built to reward volume, a powerful new capability becomes a powerful new billing engine before it becomes a discount. That is not cynicism about AI. It is pattern recognition about American health care.

The fix is not to reject the tools. It is to stop pretending the savings are automatic and start designing for them on purpose, with payment models that actually reward doing less when less is better, and with evidence measured on the total bill instead of the productivity report. Until then, the curve keeps climbing, and AI rides along on top of it.

Every tool American health care adopts learns to bill before it learns to save.
